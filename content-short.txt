The Optimizer|section
Direct. Matter-of-fact. A little proud. No warmth yet.|stage
If you've ever opened an app to check a single notification, and looked up forty minutes later with a numb thumb, a dead battery, and a vague sense of regret - there's a very real chance that was my fault.
For over a decade, I've built the apps that capture your attention.
In my world, good design removes friction. It's why you use the apps that help you and make your life better - and exactly why you can't put down the ones that make it worse.
⏸ Beat.|pause
But a few months ago, looking at where this technology is heading next, the researchers I work with and I realized something much bigger is happening.
In my world, we call what we do optimization. I've started calling it something else.
But to tell you why, I have to start at midnight. When I was eleven.

The Girl|section
Energy - warm, lit from inside.|stage
In my pink-wall bedroom, the only light was the heavy, bluish glare of my computer screen. But I was wide awake, hunched over the keyboard, typing with two fingers as quietly as I could.
I was building a website. It looked bad. And the colors… I loved lime green, and purple, and - well - every color that ever existed.
A slide flashes up behind her - the actual gloriously chaotic site, with a giant glittering animated-WordArt header at the top: "THE FIBI SHRINE." She glances back at it, smiling, then turns back to the room.|stage
Yes. "Fibi." F-I-B-I. Because when you're eleven, spelling is strictly optional.
Nobody asked me to build it. There was a very real chance that absolutely no one on Earth would ever see it. It was pure, useless play.
But then I did something strange. Without a plan, I added a group chat to the site.
That ugly, useless, glitter-soaked shrine grew a whole community of misfits. Two of the people I would die for today - the ones who know my darkest secrets, who have held me through my deepest griefs - I met on that terrible website twenty-two years ago.
⏸ Two full seconds of heavy silence. Let it sit.|pause
Let me tell you what happened to that girl. She grew up.
Somewhere along the line those neon-purple walls and glitter-star wands got painted over with "professional" beige. She was taught, very politely, that the daydream was just a distraction.
And little by little, without noticing, I lost her.

The Loss|section
Energy drops. Quiet, heavy, raw - grounded, dead-still.|stage
Somewhere in the move into the professional world, the pointless wandering stopped. I became a checkbox-ticker. And I was so, so tired.
One night, too tired to shape a raw idea I had, I asked an AI to write a strong post for me. Three days later, a friend posted something almost identical.
I saw it, and I realized: this isn't me. It isn't my voice. I had traded my voice to save thirty minutes of thinking.|punch
⏸ Slower. The lights dim, a tight spot on her.|pause
Late one night, in my kitchen, my husband and I were having one of those rare, vulnerable conversations. I said something incredibly deep about how I was feeling. He looked at me, his eyes shining, deeply touched.
And - honestly, I'm not proud of this - standing there, I realized: I didn't think those words.
Earlier that morning I'd opened a chatbot and typed in some raw thoughts. I had pocketed its beautiful sentence, carried it home, and - without ever noticing it wasn't mine - handed it to my husband as if it were a piece of my own heart.
I stood there, looking at the tears in his eyes, and I could not tell: Is it even my thoughts? My feelings?
⏸ The longest, heaviest silence in the talk. Five full seconds.|pause
My job got easier. My life got harder.
I told you I lost that girl - the one who built the fan-site. I gave her away. One easy answer at a time.|punch

The Cure|section
Energy - practical, intimate, humble; then lifting.|stage
What I just described - that slow going-flat - I finally gave it a name. The Flattening.|punch
The cure is two words. Ambiguity. And play.|punch
Here's the whole move. The not-knowing shows up - the blank page, the hard question, the gap before the answer. And your hand goes for the machine.
And you don't. You stay.
Your brain files not-knowing on the same shelf as danger - it reads uncertainty as a threat. But here's what happens if you let it ring.
There's a network in your brain that only switches on when you stop trying - when you stare out a window, when you let the gap sit.
Certainty retrieves. Ambiguity creates.|punch
The machine hands you an answer you forget by lunch. The gap hands you something you'll remember, because it cost you something to make.
Turn - play.|stage
So why the second word. Why play.
Play is the friction we don't run from - the same difficulty, with the fear taken out. That's the eleven-year-old at midnight.
Now one true thing, because I won't hand you a tidy bow. You don't practice your way to your weird, specific self. It's already in there.
But staying in the not-knowing long enough for it to come back - that is a muscle. But a muscle that atrophies is also a muscle that comes back.
Every single rep hands back exactly what the convenience took. The spark. The taste. The aliveness.
And every rep, you become a little more of the one thing the average can never be - the exception of one.

The Wall|section
Energy - the lift. Rises into something clear, even, and grounded.|stage
I sit on both sides of this thing. I help build it - I work with the labs behind the tools you used this morning.
I'm not afraid of AI. I love it. Because every one of those leaders asks me the same two questions: Where is this going? And - who do we hire now?
Why would anyone hire you in 2030?|punch
Every time a machine took the old work, it grew new work no one could have pictured. There is a wall. And it runs straight through the middle of you.|punch
Underneath, the machine works one way: it answers by probability. It is built to land on the average - the most likely answer. Give it a closed world with complete data - chess, Go, the folding of a protein - and it beats every human who ever lived. Forever.
And yes - it will take a lot of what you do. But the average is the floor. Not the ceiling.
Because two things about you were never written down - and never can be.
Proof one - the never-made.|stage
Real ideas don't start with one brilliant thought. There's a moment in your head where everything's in the air at once.
That's innovation. Not one brilliant idea - three distant things, suddenly in a new relationship. The machine is weak there - because it only has the average, and the leap is the opposite of the average.
The chaos in you - the non-linear mind, the wild associations - that's the deviation from the average. And in a world where the machine makes the average, your deviation is the most valuable thing on the market.
Proof two - the never-said.|stage
The second thing lives in your body. We know more than we can tell.|punch
You can find one friend's face in a crowd of a thousand - and never explain how. That knowing isn't in your words. It's in your body - built over a whole life of paying attention, of being a person in the world.
And that's exactly why it's safe: you cannot feed the machine what you can't put into words.
The never-made. And the never-said.
It knows everything that was written down.
You are everything that wasn't.|punch

The Close|section
Energy - focus down to the quietest, most urgent point of the night.|stage
Back to the bluish light of that bedroom. That broken website - the lime green text, the misspelled witch - wasn't the warm-up before my career. It was the peak.
Because at eleven, I was lonely. And because there was no instant escape, I had to stay in the room and sit in the friction of it.
If someone had walked into my pink-walled bedroom twenty-two years ago and handed me a screen to autocomplete my loneliness... "The Fibi Shrine" would never have existed.
Technology is going to take some of what you do. Let it.
The scary part is that we are willingly becoming more like the algorithms. More average. Flattening out. And the worst part - we are raising our kids to do the exact same thing.|punch
We are building them a world where they never have to sit in the quiet. We give them a perfect, friction-free stream of other people's thoughts - so they never have to endure the clumsy, painful work of forming their own.
We are training our own children how to not know who they are. And then we wonder why they grow up feeling replaceable.
⏸ Five full seconds. Do not rush. Let the heartbreak in the room settle.|pause
You don't find the girl who loved lime green by reading about her. You find her by staying in the room when you don't know what to say. Remembering without practicing is just nostalgia - a photo of a self you don't live in anymore.
Next time you're in the gap - sitting in front of a blank screen, trying to write an email, a spec, a post, a message to your team. And your hand goes for the chatbot.
Don't.
Stay.
Write the first line yourself. Let it be bad. Let it be clumsy. But let it come from your nervous system - not from a dataset.
So go. Go wander. Go stay in the not-knowing one beat longer than you can stand.
Because that... is the only place you were ever the exception of one.|punch
She holds the look one final, silent beat. Then turns and walks off into the dark. Three full seconds of black before the house lights slowly rise.|stage
